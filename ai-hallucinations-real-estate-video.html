What hallucination looks like

Four patterns account for most of what goes wrong in AI listing video.

Pattern 01

Invented architecture

An extra window on a blank wall. A doorway where the pantry is. A ceiling that gains a beam. The model fills in what it can't see with what it expects — and homes it expects tend to have more windows than yours.

Pattern 02

The mid-scene morph

The scene starts faithful to your photo, then drifts: counters stretch, a faucet migrates, cabinet doors multiply. Morphs hide in the middle of scenes — exactly where nobody re-watches.

Pattern 03

Phantom camera paths

Continuous "fly-through" tools generate the journey between your photos — hallways and transitions no camera ever traveled. Every one of those frames is a guess about the floor plan, rendered as fact.

Pattern 04

Detail corruption

Text on signs turns to alphabet soup. Pool water boils. Tile patterns swim. Individually small — collectively the tell that makes a buyer distrust everything else in the video.

The stakes

It's your name on it

Media that shows features the property doesn't have can constitute misrepresentation. That risk doesn't attach to the software vendor — it attaches to the licensee who published it. And the buyer who tours the home will notice.

The fix

Verification, not vibes

The answer isn't avoiding AI video — it's demanding proof. Scene-by-scene comparison against source photos, automatic rejection of drift, and receipts you can show anyone who asks.

The 5-minute audit for any AI listing video

Step 1 — Pull up the source photos next to the video. Split screen. You're not watching the video; you're comparing it.

Step 2 — Count the openings. Windows and doors, scene by scene. This catches the single most common hallucination in one pass.

Step 3 — Watch each scene to the end. Morphs live mid-scene. If a scene is six seconds, all six matter — pause anywhere something feels "off" and check it against the photo.

Step 4 — Check the surfaces buyers ask about. Countertops, appliances, flooring, fixtures. These are the details that show up in disclosure disputes.

Step 5 — Confirm your labels survived. If any photo carried a virtual-staging disclosure, it must still be visible in the video. (More on this in our MLS compliance guide.)

Hallucination FAQ

What exactly is a hallucination?
Any detail in the video that isn't in the property: invented windows, altered counters, phantom doorways, morphing fixtures. Generative models continue your photo rather than copying it — invention is the default unless something checks the output.
Why is it my problem and not the vendor's?
You're the licensee publishing the media. Misrepresentation risk, board complaints, and the awkward buyer conversation all land on the name attached to the listing.
Does Vistalia ever hallucinate?
Every generative model can drift — anyone who says otherwise is selling something. The difference is our pipeline catches drift before delivery: per-scene inspection, automatic re-renders, conservative fallbacks, a full-master sweep, and a public certificate so you can verify the result yourself.
Are continuous fly-through videos safe to use?
Understand what they are: the transitions between your photos are generated guesses about spaces the camera never captured. If you use one, audit those transition frames hardest — that's where invention is structural, not accidental.
