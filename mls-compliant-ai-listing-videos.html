What MLS rules actually require of listing media

MLS media rules differ board to board, but three requirements show up nearly everywhere. First, accuracy: listing media must fairly represent the property. Photos and video that materially alter or invent features — a window that isn't there, granite that's actually laminate, a staged room presented as furnished reality — cross from marketing into misrepresentation. Second, disclosure of virtual staging: if imagery shows digitally added furniture or finishes, most boards require it to be labeled. Third, branding rules: many MLSs require media in certain fields (like the virtual-tour link) to be unbranded — no agent name, phone number, or logo.

None of this was written with generative AI in mind, and that's exactly the problem: the rules assume the camera doesn't lie. Generative video reintroduces that possibility at scale.

Where AI video tools quietly break the rules

Generative video models don't copy your photo — they continue it. Asked to animate a kitchen, a model may extend counters that don't exist, add a window where the wall is blank, or morph a fixture mid-shot. In the industry this is called hallucination, and it's not rare; it's the default behavior of the technology unless something actively checks the output. (We wrote a full guide to spotting AI hallucinations in listing video.)

The second quiet failure is staging-label removal. If your photographer delivered virtually staged photos with the required disclosure label baked in, some tools crop or generate over the label — instantly converting compliant imagery into undisclosed virtual staging under your name.

The third is continuous "fly-through" generation, where a model invents the camera path between rooms — hallways, doorways, and transitions the camera never traveled and your photos never showed. It looks impressive; it is also, frame for frame, a guess about your floor plan presented as fact.

The five-point checklist for any AI video tool

1. Watch every frame before posting. Not the first three seconds of each scene — every frame. Hallucinations often appear mid-scene as a slow morph.

2. Compare each scene to its source photo. Count windows. Check counters, cabinet hardware, flooring transitions, and what's visible through doorways. If the tool doesn't show you scene-and-photo side by side, do it manually.

3. Check your staging labels survived. If any source photo carried a virtual-staging disclosure, confirm it's still visible in the video.

4. Match the branding rules to the destination. Branded video for social and your listing page; unbranded, neutral video for MLS fields that require it.

5. Keep evidence. If a seller, buyer, or board ever questions the media, you want proof that every scene derived from a real photo of the property.

MLS compliance FAQ

Can I use AI-generated video on the MLS?
Generally yes — if it accurately represents the property, discloses any virtual staging, and follows your board's branding rules for the field you post it in. Confirm specifics with your local MLS.
What makes an AI listing video non-compliant?
Invented or altered features (hallucination), removed virtual-staging disclosure labels, and branded media in fields that require unbranded. Accuracy failures are the serious one — that's misrepresentation, not a formatting issue.
Do I have to disclose the video is AI-generated?
It varies by board and the rules are evolving. What never varies: it must not misrepresent the property, and virtual staging must be disclosed. When in doubt, disclose — and keep proof every scene matches your photos.
